Origins & Evolution

History of the Sport

From a winter training exercise in 1950s England to an IOC-recognised sport played in over 40 countries.

The Timeline

1954

The Beginning — Southsea, England

Alan Blake of the Southsea Sub-Aqua Club invented the game, originally called "Octopush", to keep members active during cold winter months when open-water diving was impractical. The first rules were tested in a two-on-two game, with teams of eight and a pusher stick rather than today's shorter stick.

1960s

Spreading Across the UK

The sport caught on rapidly through the British Sub-Aqua Club network. Simple equipment and a pool-based format made it easy to organise and accessible to all, regardless of diving experience.

1970s

Going International

The sport spread beyond the UK, taking root in Australia, South Africa, New Zealand, Canada, and parts of Europe. Each country adapted the game slightly before international standardisation brought it all under one set of rules.

1980

First World Championship

The inaugural Underwater Hockey World Championship was held in Vancouver, Canada establishing the sport on the world stage. CMAS (Confédération Mondiale des Activités Subaquatiques) became the governing body, a role it continues to hold today.

1982

Women's Division Added

A women's division was introduced at the World Championships, reflecting the sport's naturally inclusive character. Masters and youth divisions followed in subsequent decades.

1980s–90s

Rules Standardised Globally

International bodies unified equipment dimensions, pool requirements, team sizes, and match duration — enabling truly competitive global play and paving the way for CMAS to seek wider recognition.

2000s

IOC Recognition

CMAS, as the governing body for underwater sports, gained recognition from the International Olympic Committee as an ARISF member, meaning underwater hockey is officially IOC-recognised, even though it is not yet an Olympic event. The sport has also featured at The World Games, an international multi-sport event for non-Olympic disciplines.

2019

Southeast Asian Games

Underwater hockey made its debut at the Southeast Asian Games in the Philippines, the first time the sport featured in a major multi-sport international competition, a significant milestone for its global recognition.

2023

21st World Championship

The 21st CMAS World Championships were held in Brisbane, Australia, with 38 teams from 15 nations competing. New Zealand won the elite men's title; Australia took the elite women's. The same year, CMAS restructured championships to a four-year cycle with intercontinental qualifying events, a further step towards broader international legitimacy.

Today

UCD Krakens & the Road Ahead

The Krakens are part of a growing global movement. With IOC recognition secured and the sport appearing in multi-sport international games, underwater hockey continues its journey towards greater global recognition. The next World Championships take place in 2027, with intercontinental qualifying events across Europe, Asia-Oceania, and the Americas in 2025.
