The Rulebook

How to Play

Everything you need to know about how underwater hockey is played — from the start whistle to the final goal.

The Core Rules

01

Teams

Each team consists of up to 10 players, with 6 in the water at a time. Unlimited substitutions can be made on the fly while play continues.

02

The Puck

A weighted lead puck (approximately 1.5kg) sits on the pool floor. Players use a 30cm stick to manoeuvre it towards the opposing team's goal trough.

03

Gameplay

All play happens at the bottom of the pool. Players must dive and hold their breath to engage with the puck — no standing or walking allowed.

04

Surfacing

Players may surface at any time to breathe. While on the surface, they cannot participate in play until they dive back down.

05

Scoring

A goal is scored when the puck is pushed fully into the metal trough goal along the opponent's end wall. The referee signals from the pool deck.

06

Duration

A standard match consists of two 15-minute halves with a short break in between. The team with the most goals at the end wins.

07

Fouls

Players may not obstruct opponents without the puck, use their body to block, or use the stick dangerously. Fouls result in a free push for the opposing team.

08

Equipment Rules

Each team plays with identically coloured sticks — one dark, one light — so referees can identify players. Gloves must be worn at all times.

Referees

Underwater hockey uses a unique three-referee system that reflects the underwater nature of the game.

×2

Water Referees

Two referees swim in the pool wearing full snorkelling gear, a distinctive red cap, orange gloves, and a yellow shirt. They watch play at the pool floor and signal fouls to the deck referee via hand signals.

×1

Deck Referee

One chief referee stands at the poolside. They track time, maintain the score, respond to signals from the water referees, and call fouls visible from the surface — such as too many players in the water.

CMAS Underwater Hockey Rules of Play

The official rules of the game are published by CMAS — the world governing body for underwater sports. The document covers all rules, equipment specifications, and competition procedures in full.
